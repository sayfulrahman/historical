Belfast at Its Heyday

Excerpt From the Book...

By 1917 Belfast was bustling with activity. At the center of town was the community well complete with pump and watering trough for thirsty horses, livestock, chickens, town pets and their owners. To the side of the well stood a round bandstand, one and a half stories high, where since 1912 the then famous Belfast Band played for concerts under flickering gasoline torchlight.

Cattle drives proceeding through town accompanied by a well-trained stock dog were a common occurrence of the time. John and Alec Easter were the local cattlemen (later joined by John's son, Starley Easter and his stock dog, Brownie) who bought livestock directly from the farmers, then drove the cattle or hogs on foot to market in Hillsboro or further onto Cincinnati.

"About 400 yards south of the center of town" and "located on Brush Creek" was Belfast Roller Mills operated by Edgar Williamson and his uncle, William A. Williamson. Using steam power and four-foot wood as fuel, the power generated was also used for an adjacent saw mill and planing mill. The mill was named for the rollers which crushed the wheat into a fine white flour with "by-products of bran and midlings." Corn meal and horse feed were also produced here.

Customers (mostly farmers) came "in all kinds of horse drawn vehicles, bringing in their home-grown grain and taking home the ground product." Often a customer would arrive on horseback with a two-bushel sack of grain in front of him. The mill became "a meeting place for farmers from miles around."

Belfast had two blacksmith shops. Ed Gustin had his shop south of Milligan's store where he shoed horses and "was a wonder at working hot iron." Williamson relates that "many an hour was spent by the youngsters of the town watching him pull the white-hot iron from the forge and hammer it into everything from tools to plowshares" and he was a great story teller.

The other shop was located east of the Odd Fellows Hall and was operated by David (Mitt) Hottinger. He shoed horses and reworked broken and damaged buggy wheels that required iron work and welding. Mitt excelled as a fiddler and once won a fiddlin' contest hands down with the playing of a tune called "Jumpin' Toothache."

Belfast had its share of good carpenters, a barber, a dedicated doctor and the ever necessary undertaker who usually made the wooden coffins used in his business, himself.

Dr. T.W. Roberts (T. for Thomas) had his office next to his home, which stood across the street from the Odd Fellows Hall. "He was the typical country doctor and his rig pulled by his faithful horse was to be seen in all hours of the day and night traveling the local roads through snow and high water to administer to the sick," (there must be a special place in heaven for the old-fashioned country doctor). (10) In time he replaced his horse and rig for a Maxwell automobile which soon gained the reputation around town of being a "cantankerous" car "with a mind of its own." Dr. Roberts discovered a treatment for blood poisoning involving the use of an antiseptic oil (eupinol) and cured many patients in his day.

Among the other town occupations was broom making, a creamery and a hack line operated by Cliff Calvert. He used a wagon and horses for a one trip per day run from Belfast to Hillsboro. He later changed to using a Model T truck to haul the freight.

About this time an electric generating plant was constructed in the township that brought the first electric lights for Belfast businesses and residents.

The main businesses in town were the three general stores grouped around the town's center where the bandstand stood by the well.

The store to the south sold groceries, notions and sundry supplies but it served mainly as the Belfast Post Office. Cecil Milligan ran the store and he also officiated as Postmaster. The mail was brought into Belfast from Hillsboro (11 miles away) by a two horse rig which arrived each day before noon. "The mail was sorted and taken on the rural route by Bill Nolan." In time the business was sold to Owen P. Lyle "who continued to operate it and the post office."

On the corner at the intersection stood the store owned by John W. Hurst. He operated it with one clerk, Tom McGuire for many years and sold all kind of staples and dry goods. Around Christmas the interior took on the glow of Yuletide with a dazzling display of decorations, gifts and toys. Later when automobiles gained in number and popularity, the store was sold to Chester Kier who remodeled it into a repair shop and garage.

Standing to the north of the other stores and facing the Belfast Hotel (home of the Swongers) was the general store owned and operated by Lon Swonger. Since the elderly Mr. Gilmore's retirement from the business, Lon had expanded the store's merchandise to include just about everything needed by the local community. Besides the sale of groceries his stock included clothing, shoes, dry goods, hardware, paints, automobile accessories, stoves, galvanized roofing, seed, fertilizer, wire fencing, feed for livestock, oil, gasoline, kerosene and even dynamite. By now, Lon's business affairs also included lumbering, but his main concern was in finding desirable items of merchandise for the store from the surrounding area (and on occasion further afield).

A customer upon entering the store would note that the floor was of tongue-grooved oak ( which was typical of the retail stores of the time). It was routinely brushed down with oil (applied by a long-handled brush or mop) to preserve the wood; this also kept down the dust making it easier to sweep.

Below the wall-shelves of merchandise and groceries were bins containing various kinds of beans, sugar (white and brown), salt, flour, and rice. Pickles and crackers came loose in large barrels.

Toward the back of the store stood a giant ice box that took a half of a huge block of ice (it was not electric) where bacon, baloney, cheese, butter, milk and cold bottles of soda pop were kept. Off to the side was a door leading into a large storeroom where the first thing to the right was the salt fish barrel with a lid on top and a pointed wooden stick for removing fish from the barrel. The fish had to be soaked in water overnight before cooking and were eaten both as a breakfast or dinner staple.

A long counter in front of the back wall displayed a scale over which hung a sizable bunch of bananas, next a hand-operated meat slicer, then a coffee grinder with the coffee beans in a bin nearby. Back here near the ice box was also kept a "hoop of cheese," longhorn wrapped in cheese cloth which came in a round wooden container. The cheese had a red protective film and was cut off in wedges so was often called "redskin cheese."

To the left of the front door were three glass showcases with room to walk between them and behind. The first was full of candy and gum. The second held an array of tobacco products such as pipes, cigarettes and cigars with canned tobacco (Prince Albert and Sir Walter Raleigh) displayed nearby on a back shelf. The third showcase contained clothing items such as socks and handkerchiefs along with pocket watches, lighters and other small miscellaneous items. A good display of pocket knives sat atop the counter.

The wall shelves behind the glass cases held boxes of shoes, overshoes and boots, along with stacks of overalls, shirts, jackets, coats and underwear. The cash register was on a wall counter between the second and third show cases. The store's wall phone (an old type with a crank handle) was located back on the corner wall. A common prank of the time was to receive a call from a tobacco customer who would ask, "Do you have Prince Albert in the can?" When the reply was, "Yes, we do . . . " the caller would rejoin, "Well, let him out!"

Beyond the show cases toward the rear of the store sat a big "mogul" potbellied, coal stove that amply provided luxuriating warmth on chilly, damp or downright cold days and nights. Arranged around the stove were some wooden rounded-backed chairs along with a few makeshift seats constructed from crates and old boxes. Down on the floor, two or three wooden ash boxes were placed there for spitting, along with a wide-mouth cuspidor (spittoon) for the tobacco chawers. The locals (generally men) met here at nights to share local news and discuss what was happening in the world.

A big room above the store served for a time as a meeting place for the Knights of Pythias Lodge and by the Grange (both active men's lodges of that day). Occasionally (most likely initiation nights), the shenanigans upstairs would sound close to bringing down the ceiling upon all below, and as an old timer recounts it, one of the idlers around the stove would remark to his group, "I wonder who is riding the goat tonight?"

Later with the passage of time, this upstairs area would one day be used for storage of Christmas toys and other items, and two young brothers would carve their initials in the wooden door guarding the stairway to the room above and its treasures. But in 1917 I was still but a twinkle in my bachelor father's eye (as the saying goes . . . ), and his busy merchant's life left no room for romance, much less marriage . . . or so it appeared.
